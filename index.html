Section 01

What Is Lean Management?

Lean management is one of the most influential operations philosophies of the 20th and 21st centuries. Born from the factory floor and refined into a comprehensive organisational discipline, it asks a deceptively simple question: what truly adds value for the customer, and what does not?

Core Definition

Lean management is an operations philosophy that seeks to maximise customer value by systematically eliminating waste and continuously improving processes, flow, and quality.

The analogy used to introduce lean is instructive: a lean person is healthy — not emaciated, but free of excess. A lean firm is the same. It carries no unnecessary inventory, motion, waiting, or rework. Every activity either adds value or is a target for elimination.

Key Exam Sentence: A process is not good because people are busy. A process is good when it delivers flow, quality, timeliness, and profit.

The Five Performance Objectives

Before diving into lean's tools, you must understand what operations compete on. The course identifies five performance objectives. Lean primarily addresses cost, quality, speed, and dependability — but done well, it enhances all five.

C

Cost

Doing things productively — reducing resource consumption per unit of output.

Q

Quality

Doing things right — meeting or exceeding customer specifications, first time.

S

Speed

Doing things fast — minimising the time between order and delivery.

D

Dependability

Doing things on time — reliably meeting promised delivery schedules.

F

Flexibility

Ability to change — adapting to shifts in volume, mix, or product design.

Section 02

The Anatomy of Waste

The concept of muda — the Japanese word for waste — is central to lean. Waste is defined not from the producer's perspective but from the customer's: any activity that consumes resources without adding value is waste.

Operational Definition

Waste is any activity that consumes time, resources, or space without creating value that the customer is willing to pay for.

The course identifies six primary categories of waste, each with concrete operational examples:

🏭

Overproduction

Making more than is needed. Extra copies printed; products manufactured before demand exists.

🚚

Transport

Moving parts or materials unnecessarily. Spare parts routed through multiple warehouses.

⏳

Waiting

Idle time in a process. Files queued for approval; supervisory delays stalling output.

📦

Inventory

Excess stock held. Seasonal products warehoused at the wrong time; WIP piling between stations.

✗

Defects

Errors requiring rework. Incorrect customer addresses; components failing quality check.

🏃

Motion

Unnecessary physical movement. Running across a building for the photocopier.

Remember: Lean does not demand zero inventory or zero movement — it demands that every unit of resource consumed must justify itself through value delivered to the customer.
Section 03

A Brief History of Lean

Lean did not emerge from a single insight. It developed across a century of industrial evolution, from the assembly lines of Detroit to the production halls of Toyota City.

Henry Ford — Early 20th Century

Introduced mass production, standardisation, and the moving assembly line. Established the foundational logic of continuous flow in manufacturing.

Taiichi Ohno & Toyota Production System — 1950s–70s

Ohno systematised waste elimination at Toyota, developing the Toyota Production System (TPS). Introduced just-in-time production, pull systems, jidoka (automation with a human touch), and the kanban method.

Womack & Jones — 1990s

Published The Machine That Changed the World (1990) and Lean Thinking (1996), coining the term "lean" and diffusing TPS principles to a global audience beyond automotive manufacturing.

Broader Application — 2000s–Present

Lean principles confirmed effective across sectors: healthcare, construction, financial services, software (Agile), logistics, and public administration. Now a universal management philosophy.

Section 04

Push vs Pull Systems

One of lean's most consequential distinctions is between push and pull production logic. The difference determines how material flows through a system and how responsive operations are to real demand.

⟶ Push System

  • Production driven by forecast
  • Output "pushed" downstream regardless of downstream readiness
  • Creates inventory buffers between stations
  • Risk of overproduction when forecasts are wrong
  • Example: traditional batch manufacturing
  • Higher WIP (work-in-progress) levels

⟵ Pull System

  • Production triggered by actual downstream demand
  • No work begins until the next stage requests it
  • Reduces unnecessary inventory and WIP
  • Aligns output to real customer need
  • Example: kanban-controlled lines
  • Exposes problems rather than hiding them in stock
Lean preference: Pull systems are preferred because they prevent overproduction — the most fundamental form of waste — and force the process to respond to genuine demand rather than projected demand.
Section 05

The Three Main Improvement Levers

Lean improvement is not abstract. The course identifies three concrete operational levers — derived from the Lean Management Game — that produce measurable results when applied systematically.

LEVER 01

Layout Improvement

Movement between workstations is transport waste. Redesigning layout to reduce travel distance and improve flow eliminates non-value-adding movement and shortens cycle times. Cells should be arranged in the sequence of operations, minimising handoff distances.
LEVER 02

Batch Size Reduction

Large batches reduce per-unit setup cost but create more WIP, longer lead times, and reduced flexibility. Moving toward smaller batches — ideally batch size 1 — reduces WIP in the system, allows faster detection of quality issues, and improves responsiveness to demand variation.
LEVER 03

Line Balancing

An unbalanced line creates bottlenecks where one station cannot keep up with takt time while others sit idle. Line balancing redistributes work content across stations so that each operates at roughly the same pace, eliminating waiting and enabling smoother flow.
Takt Time is the available production time divided by customer demand. It defines the required pace of production. Balancing a line means matching each station's cycle time to takt time.

Takt Time vs Cycle Time vs Lead Time

Three time-related concepts appear constantly in lean operations. They are frequently confused in exams — yet each measures something fundamentally different. Mastering the distinction between them is essential for analysing any production system.

① Takt Time
The heartbeat
of the line

Takt time is set by the customer. It answers: how often must the line produce one unit to exactly match demand? It is a target imposed from outside — you do not choose it, the market does.

Takt Time =
Available Production Time
────────────────────
Customer Demand (units)

e.g. 480 min ÷ 60 units = 8 min/unit

② Cycle Time
The actual pace
of the process

Cycle time is set by the process. It is the time actually elapsed between completing one unit and the next at a given station. It reflects reality — equipment speed, worker pace, setup times — not the ideal.

Cycle Time =
Time to complete one unit
at a given workstation
(observed / measured)

e.g. Station 2 takes 11 min/unit → bottleneck

③ Lead Time
The total journey
end to end

Lead time is what the customer experiences. It is the total elapsed time from order placement (or raw material entry) to finished goods delivery — including all waiting, queuing, and processing time across the whole system.

Lead Time =
Order received → Order delivered
────────────────────
Includes ALL wait + process time

e.g. Order placed Monday → delivered Friday = 4 days

Concept Who sets it? What does it measure? Lean target
Takt Time The customer (via demand rate) Required output pace — how often the line must produce one unit to match demand Use as the design target for line balancing
Cycle Time The process (equipment, labour, setup) Actual time between completing consecutive units at a workstation Cycle time = Takt time at every station (balanced line)
Lead Time The system (all delays, queues, processes) Total elapsed time from order entry to delivery — includes all wait and processing time Minimise by reducing WIP and wait time (Little's Law)

Three Rules to Remember

Rule 1 — Bottleneck: If any station's cycle time exceeds takt time, the whole line falls behind demand. That station is the bottleneck and must be the first improvement target.
Rule 2 — Little's Law: Lead Time = WIP ÷ Throughput Rate. To cut lead time, reduce WIP (smaller batches, pull systems) or increase throughput (remove bottlenecks).
Rule 3 — Idle waste: If a station's cycle time is much less than takt time, it will be idle — a different kind of waste. Perfect balance has every station's cycle time equal to takt time.
Key Exam Sentence

Takt time is external and prescriptive — set by customer demand. Cycle time is internal and descriptive — set by process reality. Lead time is systemic — the total customer experience from order to delivery. Lean aims to align cycle time to takt time and minimise lead time by eliminating wait.

Section 08

The Kraljic Matrix

Developed by Peter Kraljic in 1983, the Kraljic Matrix is one of the most widely used tools in supply chain and procurement strategy. It helps operations managers decide how to manage each category of purchased input — not all suppliers deserve the same attention or relationship type.

Core Purpose

The Kraljic Matrix classifies a firm's purchases along two dimensions — profit impact and supply risk — and prescribes a distinct sourcing strategy for each of the four resulting quadrants.

The Two Axes

Every purchase a firm makes can be positioned on a 2×2 grid according to:

  • Profit Impact (vertical): How significantly does this input affect the firm's cost base, revenue, or product quality? High-impact items are those where changes in price or availability materially affect the bottom line.
  • Supply Risk / Complexity (horizontal): How difficult is it to source this item reliably? Risk rises with few suppliers, high switching costs, geographic concentration, or technical complexity.

The Four Quadrants — Strategy Guide

🖱️

Non-Critical

Low profit · Low risk. Automate and streamline. Use e-procurement, catalogue buying, or click-and-buy. Minimise admin overhead — the goal is efficiency, not relationship management.

⚖️

Leverage

High profit · Low risk. Use your buying power. Run competitive tenders, play suppliers off against each other, and negotiate hard on price and terms.

🔒

Bottleneck

Low profit · High risk. Security of supply is paramount — cost is secondary. Lock in long-term contracts, build safety stocks, and qualify backup suppliers where possible.

🤝

Strategic

High profit · High risk. Invest in deep, long-term partnerships. Share forecasts, co-develop products, align incentives, and manage the relationship as a strategic asset.

Key Exam Sentence: The Kraljic Matrix prevents firms from applying the same sourcing logic to every purchase. A one-size-fits-all procurement approach either wastes resources on routine items or under-invests in strategic ones.

How Items Can Move Across the Matrix

Positions in the matrix are not fixed. A bottleneck item can be moved toward non-critical if the firm invests in qualifying new suppliers or redesigning the product to use more standard components. A leverage item can drift toward strategic if the firm becomes dependent on a single preferred supplier. Actively managing these movements is part of good procurement strategy.
